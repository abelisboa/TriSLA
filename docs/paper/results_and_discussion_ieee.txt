RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

This section presents the experimental evidence for TriSLA using only validated artifacts from the final figure package (Fig. 1–7). The objective is to answer the core research question of this work: whether a slice-aware, multi-domain, explainable, and governance-enabled decision pipeline can provide operationally meaningful SLA admission behavior under realistic variability. The analysis is grounded in the campaign dataset `dataset_final_v9.csv` (n=90), the correlation summary `multidomain_correlation.csv`, the explainability ranking `xai_feature_ranking.csv`, and the on-chain revalidation file `governance_revalidation.csv`.

1) Introduction to the Results

The evaluated dataset contains 90 real submissions distributed uniformly across the three target slice classes: URLLC (n=30), eMBB (n=30), and mMTC (n=30). The campaign design combines three operational regimes, each with 10 repetitions per slice: R1 (favorable), R2 (differentiated stress), and R3 (saturated). The measured decision outcomes are `ACCEPT`, `RENEGOTIATE`, and `REJECT`, with associated continuous `decision_score` values and multi-domain telemetry fields including `prb_utilization`, `transport_rtt_ms`, `transport_jitter_ms`, and `core_cpu`.

From the aggregate decision distribution, the global class totals are 30 ACCEPT, 19 RENEGOTIATE, and 41 REJECT. This class spread is important because it demonstrates non-trivial decision diversity, avoiding degenerate all-accept or all-reject behavior in the core campaign. In addition, scientific validation logs confirm that all figures used in this section passed rendering and consistency checks, while still preserving noted limitations (e.g., point overlap in dense scatter patterns and reduced mMTC latency sensitivity).

2) Global System Behavior (Fig. 1 and Fig. 2)

Figure 1 summarizes the regime-level transition pattern. In R1 (favorable), all three slices are accepted (10/10 each), yielding 30 ACCEPT outcomes. In R2 (differentiated), URLLC shifts primarily to RENEGOTIATE (9) with one REJECT, while eMBB and mMTC become fully REJECT (10 each). In R3 (saturated), URLLC and eMBB remain fully REJECT (10 each), whereas mMTC is fully RENEGOTIATE (10). This transition path reveals a strong nonlinearity in admission behavior across operating conditions, which is precisely the expected property of preventive SLA governance: graceful acceptance in favorable conditions and protective refusal or renegotiation under stress.

Figure 2 quantifies the global coupling between RAN load and decision score. The observed Pearson coefficient between `prb_utilization` and `decision_score` is r=0.8964565724175686 (p≈0). This is a high-magnitude positive association and indicates that PRB pressure is a dominant driver of score escalation at the global level. In practical terms, as radio-domain occupancy increases, the decision system consistently moves toward less permissive outcomes. Because score is the continuous backbone of the final decision layer, this strong relationship supports the claim that TriSLA is not only thresholding static templates but reacting to live resource pressure.

The joint reading of Fig. 1 and Fig. 2 provides the macro-level narrative: the ACCEPT→RENEGOTIATE→REJECT transition is not random drift; it is structurally linked to measurable operational stress, with PRB emerging as a principal explanatory variable.

3) Slice-Aware Analysis (Fig. 3A, Fig. 3B, Fig. 3C)

Figure 3 decomposes latency sensitivity by slice, using `transport_rtt_ms` versus `decision_score` for each service class. The three panels show materially different behaviors.

For URLLC (Fig. 3A), the relationship is moderately negative and statistically significant: r=-0.456405461470117, p=0.011241169588956046 (n=30). For eMBB (Fig. 3B), the negative relation is stronger: r=-0.5176181906180042, p=0.003394864982924073 (n=30). Both slices therefore exhibit latency-linked score variation, with eMBB presenting the larger effect size in this specific dataset snapshot.

For mMTC (Fig. 3C), latency coupling is weak and statistically non-significant: r=0.07736315049691067, p=0.6844926614073645 (n=30). This is a critical empirical result, not a defect: it indicates that mMTC decisions are not predominantly governed by transport RTT in this campaign. The contrast across Fig. 3A/B/C is exactly the type of slice-aware heterogeneity expected in a semantically differentiated orchestration framework.

Two implications follow. First, TriSLA does not enforce a monolithic transport policy across slice classes; it manifests class-dependent sensitivity profiles. Second, the weak mMTC latency relation motivates the need to inspect other domains (RAN/Core and explainability factors), rather than forcing a single-latency interpretation for all services.

4) Resource-Driven Decision Dynamics (Fig. 4A, Fig. 4B, Fig. 4C)

Figure 4 isolates PRB influence per slice. The resulting correlations are very high in all three classes: URLLC r=0.9437100699566795 (p=5.551115123125783e-15), eMBB r=0.8451902093671656 (p=4.204010473074504e-09), and mMTC r=0.9943840949117057 (p≈0), each with n=30.

These values strongly support the thesis that RAN saturation is a first-order determinant of score behavior, not only globally (Fig. 2) but within each slice partition. The practical meaning is that, regardless of service profile, admission quality is tightly bounded by radio resource contention when contention becomes pronounced.

At the same time, this does not contradict slice awareness. Slice awareness is reflected by how multiple domain signals are combined and by where each slice shows additional sensitivity (e.g., transport in Fig. 3A/B versus weak mMTC latency coupling in Fig. 3C). In other words, PRB dominance and slice differentiation can coexist: one variable may remain globally powerful, while secondary sensitivities differ by class.

Thus, Fig. 4 is the strongest quantitative evidence for the “resource-driven” component of TriSLA’s decisioning pipeline.

5) Multi-Domain Impact (Fig. 5)

Figure 5 consolidates the global domain-level correlation landscape against `decision_score` using `multidomain_correlation.csv`. The measured Pearson coefficients are:
- PRB utilization: +0.8964565724175685
- Transport RTT: -0.2098387665966931
- Transport jitter: -0.09798151541987193
- Core CPU: -0.05799116494929054
- Core memory: not available (missing in correlation output)

This ordering clearly positions RAN pressure as the strongest measurable contributor in the current run. Transport terms are present but weaker in global aggregation, and core CPU is marginal. The missing `core_memory` correlation is itself informative: not every candidate metric remained analytically stable/complete in the exported correlation table, which is a data-quality boundary that should be acknowledged.

From a systems perspective, Fig. 5 does not negate multidomain operation; instead, it quantifies relative explanatory strength among available indicators under this experiment. The data indicate a dominant RAN regime with secondary transport influence and minimal core effect in the aggregated view.

6) Model Explainability (Fig. 6)

Figure 6 ranks explanatory factors using `xai_feature_ranking.csv`, derived from `top_factors_json` observed in runtime outputs. The ordered mean contributions are:
1. `packet_loss`: 0.135 (sample_count=20)
2. `throughput`: 0.10246812500000005 (sample_count=80)
3. `latency`: 0.044999999999999984 (sample_count=40)
4. `network_bandwidth_available`: 0.033750249999999996 (sample_count=40)
5. `cpu_utilization`: 0.013142857142857152 (sample_count=70)
6. `memory_utilization`: 0.013000000000000006 (sample_count=20)

The ranking highlights two major observations. First, transport/service-quality and capacity-oriented features (`packet_loss`, `throughput`, `latency`) carry the largest average explanatory weight in the exported top-factor stream. Second, compute-side indicators (CPU and memory) appear with substantially smaller average contributions in this specific evidence set.

This is consistent with the multidomain correlation profile: the decision layer is primarily reacting to radio and service-delivery conditions, while core-side pressure contributes less in the observed operating window. Importantly, explainability here is grounded in factors actually emitted by the system outputs and not post-hoc synthetic attribution.

7) SLA Enforcement Validation (Fig. 7)

Figure 7 addresses the governance pathway with on-chain evidence from `governance_revalidation.csv` (n=3). All three revalidated submissions satisfy all blockchain criteria:
- `tx_hash` present: 3/3
- `block_number` present: 3/3
- `bc_status=COMMITTED`: 3/3

The records also include concrete transaction and block references (e.g., blocks 3395745, 3395749, 3395753), confirming that accepted decisions in this validation window are auditable in ledger form. This closes the loop between decision output and governance persistence, which is a key differentiator versus purely heuristic admission systems.

Conceptually, Fig. 7 demonstrates that TriSLA’s enforcement is not limited to in-memory decisioning; it can produce externally verifiable artifacts tied to runtime decisions. In deployment terms, this supports accountability and post-hoc auditability requirements expected in SLA-critical infrastructures.

8) Limitations

Despite strong results, several limitations should be explicitly recognized.

First, mMTC latency coupling is weak (Fig. 3C: r=0.07736315049691067, p=0.6844926614073645), so transport RTT should not be interpreted as a universal per-slice driver. This is not a failure of the framework but a boundary condition of the current sample and operating regime.

Second, governance validation uses a small revalidation subset (n=3). Although all three entries are committed and fully traceable, stronger statistical confidence for blockchain behavior would require a larger post-fix campaign.

Third, dense scatter clouds required overlap-aware handling in the strict validation workflow. Point overlap was repeatedly flagged in auto-checks and mitigated through visualization strategies (e.g., density-oriented rendering in iterative validation), indicating that visual separability can be sensitive to metric clustering even when correlations are strong.

Fourth, explainability ranking was built from available top-factor outputs and contains six factors in the final table. While valid and traceable, richer factor cardinality and per-slice attribution density would further strengthen interpretability claims.

Finally, `core_memory` correlation was unavailable in the exported multidomain table and therefore not included in the quantitative comparison of Fig. 5.

9) Final Synthesis

The collected evidence supports the central TriSLA claim: SLA admission can be made slice-aware, multidomain, explainable, and governance-verifiable in a single operational pipeline.

At the system level, regime transitions (Fig. 1) and global PRB-score coupling (Fig. 2, r≈0.896) show coherent preventive behavior under increasing stress. At the slice level, latency effects are differentiated (Fig. 3A/B significant negative coupling, Fig. 3C weak), demonstrating non-uniform sensitivity by service class rather than one-size-fits-all control. Resource dependence is strongly confirmed by per-slice PRB correlations (Fig. 4A/B/C, all high-magnitude and significant), while multidomain aggregation (Fig. 5) clarifies relative driver strength in this dataset. Explainability outputs (Fig. 6) expose which factors most frequently shape decisions, and governance checks (Fig. 7) provide concrete on-chain enforcement evidence.

Therefore, the results indicate that TriSLA does not merely classify static intents; it operationalizes adaptive decision-making tied to live resource conditions, slice semantics, and auditable enforcement. This integrated behavior is the main contribution of the framework and directly addresses the research objective of moving from isolated SLA prediction to end-to-end SLA governance.

In practical terms, the validated figures show that TriSLA can (i) preserve acceptance in favorable regimes, (ii) degrade conservatively under stress through renegotiation/rejection, (iii) maintain slice-specific sensitivity profiles, and (iv) bind accepted outcomes to verifiable blockchain traces. Within the limitations above, this constitutes a robust empirical basis for the proposed architecture at IEEE publication level.
